Twisting and \(p\)-adic heights.

On 11/12 we defined the \(\QQ\)-pro-unipotent completion. Now say a little more about the \(\QQ_p\)-pro-unipotent completion.

\begin{equation*} \ZZ_p\lb \lb \pi _1^\et (X_{\overline \QQ}, b)_{\QQ_p} \rb \rb = \varprojlim \ZZ_p\lb \pi _1^\et(X_{\overline \QQ}, b)\rb /N \end{equation*}

where the limit is over all finite quotients of \(p\)-power order.

Let \(I\) denote the augmentation ideal of

\begin{equation*} \QQ_p \otimes \ZZ_p\lb \lb \pi _1^\et (X_{\overline \QQ}, b)_{\QQ_p} \rb \rb\text{.} \end{equation*}

Define the algebra

\begin{equation*} A_n^\et (b) = \QQ_p \otimes \ZZ_p\lb \lb \pi _1^\et (X_{\overline \QQ}, b)_{\QQ_p} \rb \rb / I^{n+1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Fix a correspondence \(Z\in\Pic(X\times X)\) and let \(U_Z\) denote the corresponding quotient of \(U_2^\et\text{.}\)

Definition 2.111.

The mixed extension \(A_Z(b) \) is the pushout of \(A_2^\et(b)\) by

\begin{equation*} \operatorname{cl}_Z^* \colon \coker(\QQ_p(1) \xrightarrow{\cup^*} V^{\otimes 2}) \to \QQ_p(1)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Remark 2.112.
\begin{equation*} A_Z(b) \in M_f(G_T, \QQ_p, V, \QQ_p(1)) \end{equation*}

with respect the \(I\)-adic filtration.

Recall: \(\Sel(U)\) is defined using nonabelian cohomology, satisfying certain conditions. Our goal is to get an equation for

\begin{equation*} X(\QQ_p)_U \end{equation*}

and use Nekovář's construction to define a map

\begin{equation*} \Sel(U) \to \QQ_p\text{.} \end{equation*}

A natural analogue of Nekovář's construction is to start with the input of a cohomology class \(\xi \) in \(H^1(G_T, \QQ_p)\) and define at all bad primes \(v\) an algebraic function

\begin{equation*} H^1(G_{\QQ_v}, U) \to \QQ_p \end{equation*}

which when restricted to

\begin{equation*} H^1(G_{\QQ_v}, \QQ_p(1)) \end{equation*}

is the cup product with \(\xi \text{.}\) Given a splitting of the Hodge filtration can do this. But to get equality for Selmer varieties better to have a construction with linearity properties analogous to the global height pairing. So here we describe how to embed \(\Sel(U)\) into

\begin{equation*} H_{st}^1(G_T, \ldots) \subseteq H^1(G_t, \ldots) \end{equation*}

via twisting. Then apply Nekovář's construction giving local functions from

\begin{equation*} \Sel(U) \to \QQ_p\text{.} \end{equation*}

For more on twisting see Serre, Galois Cohomology [79].

Let \(G\) be a profinite group \(U\) a \(G\)-group. Let \(V\) be a continuous representation of \(G\) with an equivariant action of \(U\text{.}\) Then for any \(G\)-equivariant \(U\)-torsor \(P\) and also \(U\) acts on \(V\) on the left. We form the twist of \(V\) by \(P\) as follows, let \(\sim\) denote the equivalence.

\begin{equation*} (p,v) \sim (p\cdot u, u\inv v) \end{equation*}

on \(P\times V\) for all \(u \in U\text{.}\) Then the twist of \(V\) by \(P\)

\begin{equation*} V^{(P)} = P\times_U V / \sim \end{equation*}

has a continuous \(G\)-representation structure.

Remark 2.113.

For all \(p\in P\) the map

\begin{equation*} v \mapsto pv \end{equation*}

is a bijection of \(V\) onto \(V^{(P)}\) for this reason we say that this is the twist of \(V\) by \(P\text{.}\)

Now define maps

\begin{equation*} \tau \colon H_f^1(G_T, U_Z) \to M_f(G_T, \QQ_p, V, \QQ_p(1)) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} P \mapsto P \times_{U_Z} A_Z(b) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \tau_p \colon H_f^1(G_T, U_Z) \to M_f(G_{\QQ_p}, \QQ_p, V, \QQ_p(1)) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} P \mapsto P \times_{U_Z} A_Z(b) \end{equation*}
Remark 2.114.

Can show that \(\tau \) is injective.

Definition 2.115.

Let \(x\in X(\QQ)\) and \(P \in \pi _1^\et(X_{\overline \QQ}, b, x)\) then

\begin{equation*} A_Z(b,x) = \tau (\lb P\rb )= P\times_{U_Z} A_Z(b)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Remark 2.116.

For \(x\in X(\QQ_p)\text{,}\) similarly.

\begin{equation*} A_Z(b,x) = \tau _p(\lb P\rb ) \end{equation*}

is the mixed extension of \(G_{\QQ_p}\)-modules obtained by twisting \(A_Z(b)\text{.}\) Can also define \(A_1(b,x), IA_Z(b,x)\) by twisting \(A_1^\et(b)\) and \(IA_Z(b)\) respectively.

Now we can define our quadratic Chabauty pair. We havee

\begin{equation*} \theta \colon X(\QQ_p) \to \QQ_p \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} x\mapsto h_p(A_Z(b,x)) \end{equation*}

and using local heights at all bad \(v \in T_0\) we define

\begin{equation*} S = \left\{ \sum_{v\in T_0} h_v(A_Z(b, x_v)) : (x_v) \in\prod_{v\in T_0} X(\QQ_v)\right\} \end{equation*}
Theorem 2.117. Kim-Tamagawa '08.

The set \(S\) is finite.

Lemma 2.118.

Let \(X\) be as before, then \((\theta , S)\) is a quadratic Chabauty pair. The endomorphism is that induced by the correspondence \(Z\text{.}\) The constant is

\begin{equation*} \lb IA_Z(b) \rb \end{equation*}

and the bilinear pairing \(B\) is the global Nekovář height.

Corollary 2.119.

If further \(X\) has everywhere potentially good reduction, then

\begin{equation*} (\theta ,S) = (h_p(A_Z(b, \cdot), \{0\}) \end{equation*}

is a quadratic Chabauty pair.
